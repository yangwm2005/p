2. Arrange figures logically, not chronologically.
3. Build progression: Observation → Function → Mechanism → Validation.
4. Every figure answers exactly one biological question.
5. Each result section ends with a biological interpretation.
6. Define one clear central claim for the whole manuscript.

Writing Guidelines

Core principles for constructing a compelling biomedical manuscript story — from framing the problem to presenting your claim.

The Core PrincipleA biomedical manuscript tells a coherent story. Readers should be able to follow your logical progression from problem to solution without reading every Methods section.
1ObservationEstablish the phenomenon
2FunctionProve biological relevance
3MechanismExplain the how and why
4ValidationConfirm clinical/in vivo significance

Why logical order matters Key principle

Figures should be ordered to answer questions in the reader's mind, not in the order you performed experiments. Ask yourself: "What does the reader need to believe before I can show them the next result?"

  • First, convince the reader the phenomenon exists (Observation).
  • Then, convince them it matters (Functional Relevance).
  • Then, explain how it happens (Mechanism).
  • Finally, show it holds in a real-world context (Validation).

Common structural mistakes

❌ Weak approach✅ Strong approach
Figures ordered by experiment dateFigures ordered by logical narrative
Jumping straight to mechanism without establishing phenomenonEstablishing the "what" before explaining the "how"
Presenting data without biological interpretationEach figure ends with a take-home message
Multiple unrelated claims across figuresAll figures converge on one central claim
Validation figure appearing in the middle of the paperValidation anchors the end of the story
🎯
One question to rule them allEvery experiment in your manuscript should trace back to a single central biological question. If you have two unrelated questions, you may need two manuscripts.

What makes a strong central biological question?

  • Addresses a gap in current knowledge, not just a gap in your data.
  • Is specific enough to be answered experimentally, but broad enough to be significant.
  • Uses precise biological language: name the molecule, pathway, cell type, or disease context.
  • Implies a measurable outcome.
  • Is interesting to your field — the answer should change how someone thinks or acts.

Examples: Weak vs. strong questions

❌ Too vague: What does protein X do?
❌ Too narrow: Does X increase in cell line Y under condition Z?
✅ Strong: Does protein X regulate NF-κB–driven inflammatory gene expression in primary macrophages during bacterial infection?
❌ Too vague: Is gene Y important in cancer?
✅ Strong: Does gene Y promote tumor cell survival through the PI3K/AKT pathway in KRAS-mutant colorectal cancer?
⚠️
Common pitfallDon't confuse a biological question with a technical question. "Can we detect X by immunofluorescence?" is a technical question. "Does X localize to the nucleus during hypoxia?" is a biological question.
🔬
Your main claim is your manuscript's thesisIt should synthesize all your figures into one declarative sentence. A reviewer should be able to read it and understand exactly what your paper proves.

Anatomy of a strong main claim

A strong main claim typically has these components:

  • The molecule/gene/pathway you studied
  • The biological action (promotes, inhibits, regulates, activates, suppresses...)
  • The downstream outcome (inflammation, apoptosis, tumor growth...)
  • The mechanistic basis (by activating, by binding, by phosphorylating...)
  • The biological context (in macrophages, in vivo, in colorectal cancer...)

Examples

❌ Weak: "Protein X is involved in inflammation."
❌ Weak: "We found that gene Y is upregulated in cancer."
✅ Strong: "Protein X promotes macrophage inflammatory activation by stabilizing NF-κB in the nucleus through direct interaction with IKKβ, and its deletion in myeloid cells protects mice from LPS-induced septic shock."
✅ Strong: "MicroRNA-21 drives colorectal cancer proliferation and chemoresistance by repressing the PTEN/PI3K/AKT axis, and its inhibition restores oxaliplatin sensitivity in patient-derived organoids."
⚠️
Test your claimCan every figure in your paper be described as "evidence for" your main claim? If a figure doesn't support the claim, either the figure doesn't belong or your claim needs to be rewritten.
✍️
Result section titles should tell the storyTitles like "Figure 2" or "Results of experiment 3" are useless. Every result section title should be a declarative sentence that states the biological conclusion.

Rules for result section titles

  • Write as a complete declarative sentence, not a question or topic label.
  • State the biological conclusion, not the technique used.
  • Include the molecule, effect, and context where possible.
  • Keep them to one sentence, around 10–20 words.
  • Titles should flow logically — reading them top to bottom should tell your whole story.

Examples: weak vs. strong titles

❌ Weak title✅ Strong title
Protein X expression analysisProtein X is upregulated in LPS-stimulated macrophages
Loss-of-function experimentsSilencing protein X attenuates TNF-α and IL-6 secretion
Mechanism studiesProtein X stabilizes IKKβ to sustain NF-κB nuclear retention
In vivo dataMyeloid-specific deletion of protein X protects mice from endotoxemia

Figure Types

Each figure in your manuscript plays a distinct narrative role. Use these guidelines to decide what belongs in each type and what to write for each field.

🔵 Observation
🟢 Functional Relevance
🟡 Mechanism
🔴 Validation
🔵
Observation figures establish "the what"These figures introduce the biological phenomenon that your whole story is about. Without a compelling observation, readers have no reason to care about the rest of the manuscript.
🎯

Purpose

  • Show that a gene, protein, or pathway is altered in your condition of interest.
  • Establish correlation between your molecule and a disease or biological state.
  • Demonstrate that the phenomenon is real, reproducible, and specific.
🧪

Typical experiments

  • qPCR / Western blot comparing control vs. treated
  • IHC / IF showing expression in tissue
  • TCGA / GEO database correlation analysis
  • Patient sample expression data
  • Single-cell or bulk RNA-seq differential expression
✍️

What to write in each field

  • Biological question: "Is X dysregulated in [disease/condition]?"
  • Main finding: "X is significantly elevated in Y compared to Z (p<0.01, n=5)"
  • Take-home message: "X is a condition-specific marker that warrants functional investigation."

Common pitfalls in Observation figures

  • Showing expression in only one cell line — always include primary cells or patient samples.
  • Reporting only fold-change without statistical analysis.
  • Not justifying why the change is biologically meaningful (e.g., what threshold matters?).
  • Including too many molecules — one observation figure should focus on one molecule/pathway.
Strong take-home message example: "Protein X is consistently upregulated (3–5 fold) in inflamed synovial tissue from RA patients compared to healthy donors, suggesting a disease-associated role in joint inflammation."
🟢
Functional relevance figures establish "the why it matters"You've shown the phenomenon exists. Now prove it is causally important — not just correlated. This is where gain- and loss-of-function experiments live.
🎯

Purpose

  • Demonstrate that your molecule drives (or suppresses) a biological outcome.
  • Establish causality — not just correlation.
  • Show that gain or loss of function has measurable biological consequences.
🧪

Typical experiments

  • siRNA / shRNA knockdown phenotype assays
  • CRISPR knockout cell lines
  • Overexpression rescue experiments
  • Proliferation, apoptosis, migration, invasion assays
  • Colony formation, sphere formation assays
  • Mouse knockout or transgenic phenotypes
✍️

What to write in each field

  • Biological question: "Does X regulate [specific phenotype]?"
  • Main finding: "Knockdown of X reduces apoptosis by 60%; overexpression increases it 2-fold."
  • Take-home message: "X is required for [phenotype] and its loss is sufficient to abrogate the effect."

The importance of rescue experiments

The gold standard in functional figures is the rescue experiment: knock out X, observe phenotype change, then re-express X and show the phenotype returns to baseline. This rigorously proves causality and rules out off-target effects.

⚠️
Reviewers will ask: "Could the siRNA effect be off-target?" Always include at least two independent siRNA sequences, or a rescue experiment.
🟡
Mechanism figures explain "the how"These are the most intellectually demanding figures. You must explain the molecular pathway through which your protein exerts its effects. This is what separates a descriptive paper from a mechanistic one.
🎯

Purpose

  • Identify the molecular partner, pathway, or regulatory event.
  • Show direct physical or biochemical interaction.
  • Demonstrate that the mechanism is required for the observed function.
🧪

Typical experiments

  • Co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP)
  • Proximity ligation assay (PLA)
  • ChIP-seq / ChIP-qPCR
  • Reporter gene assays (luciferase)
  • In vitro binding / pull-down assays
  • Phosphorylation / ubiquitination site mutagenesis
  • Structural biology (cryo-EM, crystallography)
✍️

What to write in each field

  • Biological question: "By what molecular mechanism does X regulate [phenotype]?"
  • Main finding: "X directly binds IKKβ and prevents its ubiquitination-mediated degradation."
  • Take-home message: "X stabilizes IKKβ to sustain NF-κB signaling; disrupting this interaction abolishes the inflammatory phenotype."

Mechanistic depth: what reviewers expect

  • Correlation is not mechanism. Show the direct biochemical event.
  • Use domain/mutant analysis to pinpoint the interaction interface.
  • Confirm that disrupting the mechanism phenocopies loss of function.
  • Ideally, place your molecule within an existing, known pathway.
Strong mechanism take-home: "Protein X interacts with the kinase domain of IKKβ through its C-terminal coiled-coil domain, preventing K48-linked ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation. An X-ΔCCD mutant that cannot bind IKKβ fails to sustain NF-κB activity and inflammatory gene expression."
🔴
Validation figures establish "the real-world significance"All the prior in vitro work means little without in vivo or clinical validation. These figures answer: "Does this matter in a living organism or in patients?"
🎯

Purpose

  • Confirm that your in vitro findings hold in vivo or in patient samples.
  • Establish translational or clinical relevance.
  • Provide proof-of-concept for therapeutic targeting.
🧪

Typical experiments

  • Mouse disease models (KO, transgenic, xenograft, allograft)
  • Patient-derived organoids or PDX models
  • Clinical sample IHC with outcome correlation
  • Kaplan-Meier survival analysis (TCGA)
  • Drug/inhibitor treatment in animal models
  • Human biomarker cohort studies
✍️

What to write in each field

  • Biological question: "Does X regulate [phenotype] in vivo / in patients?"
  • Main finding: "Myeloid-specific X-KO mice show reduced tumor burden and improved survival."
  • Take-home message: "X is a clinically relevant target whose loss confers therapeutic benefit in vivo."

Making a strong validation argument

  • Use conditional/tissue-specific knockouts rather than global KO when possible to isolate the relevant cell type.
  • Include sample size justification and power calculations for animal studies.
  • For patient data: clearly define cohort, match for confounders, and use multivariate analysis.
  • If using pharmacological inhibitors, show target engagement (e.g., reduced phosphorylation of substrate) alongside phenotypic outcome.
Strong validation take-home: "LysM-Cre × X-flox/flox mice are protected from LPS-induced cytokine storm and display 60% improved survival compared to controls, establishing X as a myeloid-intrinsic driver of endotoxemia."

Narrative Craft

Advanced techniques for writing Results sections, connecting figures, and making your biological argument persuasive and clear.

Transitions
Results Writing
Intro & Discussion
Reviewer Logic
🔗
Transitions are the invisible backbone of your storyThe last sentence of each result section should raise the question that the next section answers. This creates narrative momentum and a sense of inevitability.

The "bridge sentence" technique

End each result section with a sentence that:

  1. Summarizes the take-home conclusion of the current figure.
  2. Raises the next logical question.
After an Observation figure: "Together, these data establish that protein X is selectively upregulated during inflammatory macrophage activation. We next investigated whether X is required for the inflammatory response or merely a byproduct of it."
After a Functional Relevance figure: "These results demonstrate that X is causally required for NF-κB target gene induction. To understand how X mediates this effect, we sought to identify its direct molecular effector."
After a Mechanism figure: "Collectively, these findings indicate that X stabilizes IKKβ to sustain NF-κB signaling. We therefore examined whether this pathway operates in vivo in a model of systemic inflammation."

Linking language to use

  • "Having established that X… we next sought to determine whether…"
  • "These findings prompted us to ask whether…"
  • "To investigate the mechanism underlying this phenotype, we…"
  • "To determine whether these in vitro observations translate to a physiological context, we…"
  • "Given that X regulates Y, we asked whether this pathway operates in vivo."
✍️
Results sections describe, then interpretFirst present what you observed (data summary), then tell the reader what it means (biological interpretation). Never leave data uninterpreted.

The 3-part results paragraph structure

  1. Setup — State the rationale: what you wanted to know and why.
  2. Data — Report what you observed, with statistics.
  3. Interpretation — State the biological conclusion that follows.
Setup: "To determine whether protein X drives macrophage inflammatory activation, we silenced X expression using two independent siRNAs in bone marrow–derived macrophages (BMDMs) and stimulated them with LPS.
Data: X knockdown reduced TNF-α mRNA by 70% and IL-6 secretion by 65% compared to scramble controls (p<0.001; Figure 2A–C).
Interpretation: These data indicate that X is required for full NF-κB–dependent inflammatory gene expression in macrophages."

Language precision tips

❌ Imprecise✅ Precise
X was increasedX mRNA was upregulated 3.2-fold (p=0.002, n=5)
X seems to regulate YX is required for Y, as its loss abrogated the response
We found that X interacts with YCo-IP confirmed that endogenous X and Y interact in macrophages
Treatment had an effect on cellsLPS treatment increased X expression 4-fold within 2 hours
The data show interesting resultsThese results demonstrate that X is required for NF-κB activation

Introduction structure 3-part funnel

  1. Broad context — Why does the field care about this biological problem? (2–3 sentences)
  2. Gap in knowledge — What is unknown, and why does it matter? (2–3 sentences)
  3. Your approach and claim — What you did, and what you found. (2–3 sentences)
Example funnel: "Macrophage-mediated inflammation drives the pathogenesis of sepsis, a life-threatening condition affecting millions annually. While NF-κB signaling is central to inflammatory gene induction, the upstream regulators that fine-tune its activity in macrophages remain incompletely characterized. Here we identify protein X as a novel stabilizer of IKKβ that is required for full inflammatory activation, and demonstrate that its myeloid-specific deletion protects mice from endotoxin-induced lethality."

Discussion structure

  1. Restate the main finding — One concise paragraph summarizing your central claim.
  2. Place it in context — How does it fit with existing literature? Does it support, extend, or contradict prior work?
  3. Mechanistic insight — Highlight the novel mechanistic contribution.
  4. Limitations — Be honest and preempt reviewer criticism.
  5. Translational implications — What does this mean for disease biology or therapy?
  6. Future directions — What questions remain open?
🔍
Think like a reviewer before you submitReviewers are experts who will probe the weakest links in your logic. Anticipating their questions is one of the most effective ways to strengthen your manuscript.

Questions reviewers always ask

  • "Is the knockdown efficient and specific? Show me efficiency data."
  • "Could the effect be due to off-target knockdown? Where is your rescue experiment?"
  • "What cell lines did you use? Are they representative of the biology?"
  • "Is there a human disease context? Why should clinicians care?"
  • "What is the endogenous expression level? Is the overexpression physiological?"
  • "How does this fit with [competing paper]? Have you compared your findings?"
  • "What is the sample size? Was the study powered sufficiently?"

The "so what?" test

After every figure, ask yourself: "So what does this mean biologically?" If you cannot answer this in one clear sentence, you need either more data or a clearer interpretation. Reviewers will reject papers where the biological significance is unclear, even if the data quality is excellent.

Final Checklist

Use these checklists before submission to ensure your manuscript tells a complete, rigorous, and compelling story.

🏗️

Overall Story Logic

  • One clear central biological question
  • One declarative main claim
  • Figures in logical, not chronological, order
  • Each figure supports the main claim
  • All four narrative stages represented (Obs → Func → Mech → Val)
📊

Each Result Section

  • Declarative title stating the conclusion
  • Rationale stated at the beginning
  • Data presented with statistics (n, p-value)
  • Biological interpretation at the end
  • Bridge sentence leading to the next figure

Observation figures ✅

  • Specificity: Shown in multiple cell types or conditions, not just one.
  • Quantification: Band intensities or transcript levels quantified and graphed.
  • Statistics: Appropriate test used; p-value and sample size reported.
  • Relevance: Includes at least one patient sample or primary cell data point.
  • Interpretation: Explains why this observation is biologically noteworthy.

Functional Relevance figures ✅

  • Two independent knockdown reagents or a rescue experiment included.
  • Knockdown efficiency shown (qPCR + Western) in the same figure or supplement.
  • Both gain- and loss-of-function shown (or clearly justified why only one).
  • Phenotype is biologically meaningful (magnitude and context discussed).
  • Appropriate controls: Scramble siRNA, empty vector, vehicle treatment.

Mechanism figures ✅

  • Direct biochemical interaction demonstrated (Co-IP, PLA, pull-down).
  • Domain mapping or mutagenesis identifies the critical interface.
  • Mutant phenocopies loss of function — disrupting the interaction abolishes the phenotype.
  • Endogenous proteins used where possible (not only overexpressed tagged versions).
  • Placed within a known pathway with upstream/downstream context.

Validation figures ✅

  • In vivo model is appropriate for the biology being studied.
  • Sample size and power calculation stated.
  • Animal experiment blinding and randomization described.
  • Patient data: Cohort defined, confounders addressed, multivariate analysis where needed.
  • Phenotype recapitulates in vitro findings — tells the same story in a living system.
🚀
Final story testRead only your result section titles in order. If they tell a coherent story from problem to solution without reading the rest of the text — your manuscript is well-structured and ready for submission.
